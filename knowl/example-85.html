Example 10.6.

A jar contains twenty marbles, of which six are red, nine are blue and the remaining five are green. Three of the twenty marbles are selected at random. 1  Let \(X=\{0,1,2,3,4,5\}\text{,}\) and for each \(x\in X\text{,}\) let \(P(x)\) denote the probability that the number of blue marbles among the three marbles selected is \(x\text{.}\) Then \(P(i)=C(9,i)C(11,3-i)/C(20,3)\) for \(i=0,1,2,3\text{,}\) while \(P(4)=P(5)=0\text{.}\) Bob says that it doesn’t make sense to have outcomes with probability zero, but Carlos says that it does.
