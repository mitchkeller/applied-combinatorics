When \(\PXP\) is a poset, it is common to write \(x\le y\) in \(P\) or \(y\ge x\) in \(P\) as substitutes for \((x,y)\in P\text{.}\) Of course, the notations \(x\lt y\) in \(P\) and \(y>x\) in \(P\) mean \(x\le y\) in \(P\) and \(x\ne y\text{.}\) When the poset \(\bfP\) remains fixed throughout a discussion, we will sometimes abbreviate \(x\le y\) in \(P\) by just writing \(x\le y\text{,}\) etc. When \(x\) and \(y\) are distinct points from \(X\text{,}\) we say \(x\) is covered by \(y\) in \(P\) 1  when \(x\lt y\) in \(P\text{,}\) and there is no point \(z\in X\) for which \(x\lt z\) and \(z\lt y\) in \(P\text{.}\) For example, in the poset \(\bfP_3=(X,R_3)\) from Example 6.3, \(d\) is covered by \(e\) and \(c\) covers \(b\text{.}\) However, \(a\) is not covered by \(f\text{,}\) since \(a\lt e\lt f\) in \(R_3\text{.}\) We can then associate with the poset \(\bfP\) a cover graph \(\mathbf{G}\) whose vertex set is the ground set \(X\) of \(\bfP\) with \(xy\) an edge in \(\mathbf{G}\) if and only if one of \(x\) and \(y\) covers the other in \(\bfP\text{.}\) Again, for the poset \(\bfP_3\) from Example 6.3, we show the cover graph on the left side of Figure 6.4.
