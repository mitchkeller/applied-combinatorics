There are occasions where the Principle of Mathematical Induction, at least as we have studied it up to this point, does not seem sufficient. Here is a concrete example. The professor asked Bob to study a function \(f(n)\) defined recursively by \(f(n) = 2f(n-1) - f(n-2)\) with \(f(1)=3\) and \(f(2)=5\text{.}\) Specifically, the professor asked Bob to compute \(f(10^{10})\text{,}\) which seems like a daunting task. Over coffee, Bob scribbled on a napkin and determined that \(f(3)=7\) and \(f(4)=9\text{,}\) and on the basis of these calculations alone, he thought that it might just be possible that \(f(n) = 2n+1\) for all \(n\geq 1\text{.}\) If this were true, he could simply report that \(f(10^{10})=2\cdot 10^{10}+1=20000000001\text{.}\)
