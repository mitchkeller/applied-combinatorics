If a single six sided die is rolled and the number of dots on the top face is recorded, then the ground set is \(S=\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}\) and \(P(i)=1/6\) for each \(i\in S\text{.}\) On the other hand, if a pair of dice are rolled and the sum of the dots on the two top faces is recorded, then \(S=\{2,3,4,\dots,11,12\}\) with \(P(2)=P(12) =1/36\text{,}\) \(P(3)=P(11)=2/36\text{,}\) \(P(4)=P(10)=3/36\text{,}\) \(P(5)=P(9)=4/36\text{,}\) \(P(6)=P(8)=5/36\) and \(P(7)=6/36\text{.}\) To see this, consider the two die as distinguished, one die red and the other one blue. Then each of the pairs \((i,j)\) with \(1\le i,j\le 6\text{,}\) the red die showing \(i\) spots and the blue die showing \(j\) spots is equally likely. So each has probability \(1/36\text{.}\) Then, for example, there are three pairs that yield a total of four, namely \((3,1)\text{,}\) \((2,2)\) and \((1,3)\text{.}\) So the probability of rolling a four is \(3/36=1/12\text{.}\)
