To account for edges, we move from the symmetric group \(S_4\) to its pair group \(S_4^{(2)}\text{.}\) The objects that \(S_4^{(2)}\) permutes are the \(2\)-element subsets of \(\{1,2,3,4\}\text{.}\) For ease of notation, we will denote the \(2\)-element subset \(\{i,j\}\) by \(e_{ij}\text{.}\) To find the permutations in \(S_4^{(2)}\text{,}\) we consider the vertex permutations in \(S_4\) and see how they permute the \(e_{ij}\text{.}\) The identity permutation \(\iota=(1)(2)(3)(4)\) of \(S_4\) corresponds to the identity permutation \(\iota=(e_{12})(e_{13}) (e_{14}) (e_{23}) (e_{24}) (e_{34})\) of \(S_4^{(2)}\text{.}\) Now letâ€™s consider the permutation \((12)(3)(4)\text{.}\) It fixes \(e_{12}\) since it sends \(1\) to \(2\) and \(2\) to \(1\text{.}\) It also fixes \(e_{34}\) by fixing \(3\) and \(4\text{.}\) However, it interchanges \(e_{13}\) with \(e_{23}\) (\(3\) is fixed and \(1\) is swapped with \(2\)) and \(e_{14}\) with \(e_{24}\) (\(1\) is sent to \(2\) and \(4\) is fixed). Thus, the corresponding permutation of pairs is \((e_{12})(e_{13}e_{23})(e_{14}e_{24})(e_{34})\text{.}\) For another example, consider the permutation \((123)(4)\text{.}\) It corresponds to the permutation \((e_{12}e_{23}e_{13})(e_{14}e_{24}e_{34})\) in \(S_4^{(2)}\text{.}\)
